# The eternal sunshine of the biased mind [Bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias) is an inclination or outlook to present or hold a partial perspective, often accompanied by a refusal to consider the possible merits of alternative points of view. A [Cognitive bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias) is a repeating or basic misstep in thinking, assessing, recollecting, or other cognitive processes. < For the sake of this talk, I'm mostly going to focus on [cognitive biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cognitive_biases), how they relate to our every day jobs and how we can mitigate their effects. I will also talk about my experience dealing with my own biases and how it relates to web components, and the struggle web components face, at large, when pitted against the status quo other offerings have set. # Our bias We see it every day: - Tabs vs spaces - OOP vs FRP - Typescript vs Flow - Web Components vs ... - vs vs vs! # Our bias > We argue because we feel so emotionally committed to our case, but there is no objectively right answer. > — [Sam Koblenski](http://sam-koblenski.blogspot.com.au/2015/04/programming-bias-comes-from-experience.html) < Most, if not all of us, have argued for, or against something. Some baises are correctly linked and some may be the result of an indirect phenomenon, possibly causing an [illusory correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illusory_correlation), which is the phenomenon of perceiving a relationship between variables even when no such relationship exists. < Similarly, if you have had a positive experience with something, you're likely to have a positive predisposition toward it. This is called the [halo effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias#Halo_effect). Negative predispositions are referred to as a [reverse halo effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_effect#Reverse-halo_effect), devil effect or horns effect. I'd venture to say this is how many of us land on a favourite framework or library: trial and error through positive and negative experiences. < Furthermore, when factual evidence is present to counter an experience, one's beliefs may still persist. This is called [belief perseverance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confirmation_bias#Persistence_of_discredited_beliefs). It's worth noting that misinformation can continue to influence inferences one generates even after a correction has been made. # Mitigation # Mitigation: making decisions A [decision matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision_matrix) is a method to systematically indentify, analyze, and rate the performance of relationships between sets of values and information. < I'd rate making decisions as generally better than not, but that doesn't mean that making a decision is inherently good. Forward movement is good. Making decisions that hamper progress is bad, unless of course your goal is to inhibit progress. < A decision matrix is a good way to help facilitate objective decision making. < A decision matrix can help to keep biases in check, but it's not immune to them. < [read definition] # My bias > Web components are the best; everything else sucks. > — Me in 2015 ... > Web components are great; and so are many other things. > — Me in 2017 Full disclosure: I'm the author of [SkateJS](https://github.com/skatejs/skatejs). < I think it's quite common for us to find something we like and stick with it.
